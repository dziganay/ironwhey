    Scion FR-S: Bringing the Sport Back to the Car

    In the midst of struggling brands and declining car sales, Scion was preparing to launch their most exciting car yet – a rear wheel drive sports coupe called the FR-S. As Scion’s halod car, the FR-S, was designed to bring performance credibility to the brand in the sports car segment and challenge top selling, iconic american muscle cars like the Chevy Camaro, the Ford Mustang and Dodge Charger. In comparison to the “heavyweights”, the Scion FR-S is a completely different kind of sports car. Its lightweight, rear wheel drive design allowed for lower horsepower and greater efficiency, and its boxer engine afforded a lower center of gravity. All of this translated to a tight, intuitive handling experience that's the polar opposite of the typical American sports car.

    As Toyota’s youth brand, Scion was coming into the sports car segment with a reputation for making vehicles known for their distinctive looks, not under-the-hood performance. In order for the Scion FR-S to succeed at launch, Scion needed to establish credibility with automotive enthusiasts while maintaining a strong relationship with Scion’s young trendsetter target. Scion’s marketing objective for the FR-S was for automotive enthusiasts to legitimatize the brand as a maker of performance vehicles, and Scion’s young trendsetter target to validate the car as a Scion.

    In order to build credibility with automotive enthusiasts, Scion drew on Toyota’s rich and successful heritage in motorsport. The Scion FR-S was the next iconic performance vehicle from Toyota, following the lineage of the AE-86. Scion and ATTIK knew that targeting automotive enthusiasts was a crucial part of building credibility for the FR-S. Broadcast and digital creative featured Scion’s drift racing guru Ken Gushi behind the wheel, showing off the FR-S’ curve-hugging performance driving capabilities, and tying the FR-S to Toyota motorsport heritage. Media placements went beyond Scion’s traditional venues, including more college and pro sports than Scion had ever included in their media mix. Subsequent spots progressively revealed more details about the car, all the way down to its unique boxer engine.
